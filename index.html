Introduction

The Internet Forgets

Between 1994 and today, hundreds of digital platforms have been born, flourished, and died. Each one was not merely a service — it was a social world: a place where people built identities, friendships, art, and communities.

When GeoCities closed in 2009, 38 million user-made pages disappeared overnight. When MySpace "migrated" its servers in 2019, an estimated 50 million songs by 14 million artists were erased with no warning. When Vine shut down in 2017, a genuinely new form of creative expression — the 6-second loop — lost its home.

This project applies the tools of Digital Humanities — curation, cultural analysis, archival practice, and critical theory — to document what was lost, why it was lost, and what it means that these decisions were made by corporations, not communities.

Our original contribution is not technical history. Wikipedia already has that. What it does not have is humanistic analysis: what aesthetics emerged on each platform? What social rituals? What economic logic ended them? These are the questions we answer.

Theoretical Framework

How We Read Ruins

Three interlocking lenses — all grounded in the course readings — shape our analysis of every platform in this archive.

∅
Enshittification

Cory Doctorow's model: platforms first serve users, then advertisers, then extract maximum value and collapse. Dead platforms are enshittification's end state — what remains when extraction is complete and the host abandoned.

Doctorow — "The Enshittification of TikTok" (Wired) · explored in class
⛏
Data Extractivism

The Cartography of Generative AI maps how user-created culture is extracted as raw material for corporate and AI systems. Dead platforms are the clearest case: users built the culture, the corporation owned the infrastructure, and when the deal ended, users got nothing back.

@tallerestampa — Cartography of Generative AI (2024) · explored in class
✦
Curation as Scholarship

The DH Manifesto 2.0 argues curation is knowledge production equal to writing. Archiving dead platforms is not nostalgia — it is a humanistic act that recovers meaning, documents culture, and challenges the corporate authority to erase history.

DH Manifesto 2.0 (2009) · explored in class
01b
The Enshittification Cycle

Platforms Are Designed to Die

Doctorow's framework describes a predictable arc. Every platform in this archive followed it without exception.

Phase One
Serve Users

The platform offers genuine value. Communities form. Culture is created. People invest their time, creativity, identity. GeoCities gives everyone a homepage. Vine gives everyone an audience.

Phase Two
Serve Advertisers

The platform optimises for advertisers over users. Algorithms change. Visibility becomes pay-to-play. The culture that made the platform valuable is gradually subordinated to engagement metrics and monetisation imperatives.

Phase Three
Extract & Collapse

The platform extracts maximum value and shuts down — or is absorbed, or pivots. Communities have no legal claim to their content, no right to their data, no say in the decision. The culture evaporates.

02
Case Studies

Six Lost Worlds

Each team member adopted one platform. Analysis follows a consistent methodology: technical history, cultural emergence, enshittification arc, and what was irretrievably lost.

1994 — 2009 · Yahoo
GeoCities
DELETED
"Build Your Own Neighborhood on the Web"

GeoCities gave ordinary people — not programmers, not corporations — the power to publish themselves online. Users organised into themed "neighborhoods": Hollywood, Athens (education), Area51 (the strange). The visual vernacular it produced — tiled backgrounds, animated GIFs, visitor counters, MIDI autoplay — was the first genuinely democratic visual language of the web. Messy, personal, alive.

When Yahoo acquired GeoCities for $3.57 billion and shut it in 2009, 38 million pages were deleted. The Archive Team saved much of the data — but the neighborhoods, the community structure, the social context: gone. GeoCities was the direct ancestor of every social media profile that followed.

Self-expression Visual vernacular Yahoo acquisition 38M pages erased Digital democracy
2003 — 2019 (music loss)
MySpace
LOST
"A Place for Friends" — and for every band that ever mattered

Before Spotify, before SoundCloud, MySpace Music was where independent musicians existed. Between 2003 and 2010, a MySpace page was not optional — it was your career. In 2019, during a server migration, MySpace announced all music uploaded before 2015 — an estimated 50 million songs by 14 million artists — had been permanently lost.

No warning. No recovery. These were not commercial recordings with backup copies. These were demos, live recordings, debut songs by artists who never released elsewhere. From a Digital Humanities perspective, this is one of the largest single acts of cultural erasure in history — performed not by censorship or disaster, but by corporate negligence.

Independent music Cultural erasure Corporate negligence 50M songs
2012 — 2017 · Twitter
Vine
CLOSED
Six seconds. A new art form. Gone.

Vine was not merely a video platform. It was the incubator of a genuinely new creative form: the 6-second loop. Within that constraint, creators developed a new comedic and narrative grammar — precise timing, visual callbacks, sound design as punchline, the loop as structure.

Vine gave rise to a generation of creators — many of them Black and brown — who invented the memes, dances, and comedic styles that TikTok and Instagram Reels later absorbed and profited from, without credit or compensation. This is data extractivism in its purest cultural form: the culture migrated, the creators did not. Twitter acquired Vine, failed to monetise it, and shut it down in 2017.

New art form Black creators Cultural extraction Loop grammar
2011 — 2019 · Google
Google+
SHUT
The platform that proved scale cannot manufacture community

Google+ is the rare case of a platform whose death teaches us what makes digital communities work. Despite Google's resources and 2 billion+ Gmail users nudged toward it, Google+ never achieved genuine community formation. The DH Manifesto 2.0 argues that collaborative culture requires communities of practice with their own standards and norms. Google+ was top-down: imposed on users rather than grown by them.

When a data breach exposed 500,000 user records in 2018, Google chose not to disclose it for seven months. The platform shut in 2019. Its obituary is a lesson in the difference between a platform and a community — and why the former cannot create the latter by decree.

Platform vs community Privacy failure Top-down design Data breach
1999 — 2013 · Microsoft
MSN Messenger
GONE
Where a generation first learned to be someone online

For an entire generation in Europe and Latin America, MSN Messenger was the internet. Before smartphones, before social media — MSN was where teenagers came home from school and immediately sat down to continue conversations, to share music files, to build the first versions of their digital selves.

The cultural practices that emerged — the carefully crafted "personal message" (precursor to the tweet), the nudge as social gesture, the display picture as identity performance — were among the first mass digital social rituals. Microsoft killed MSN in 2013 in favour of Skype. No archive was preserved. The conversations — billions of them — are gone.

Digital identity Social ritual Personal message Generation X/Y
1996 — 2020 · Adobe
Adobe Flash
KILLED
The medium that made the early web weird, creative, and alive

Flash's death was deliberate, ideological, and coordinated. Steve Jobs declared war on Flash in his 2010 "Thoughts on Flash" letter — citing technical reasons, but the subtext was platform control: Apple wanted to own the mobile creative ecosystem.

What died was an entire era of web creativity: Homestar Runner, Newgrounds, thousands of experimental interactive artworks and browser games by independent creators worldwide. These works cannot be run in modern browsers without emulators. The Ruffle emulator project and Flashpoint Archive are heroic preservation efforts — precisely the kind of guerrilla curation the DH Manifesto 2.0 calls for. Flash's death raises the hardest question in digital preservation: what do you do when the format itself becomes illegal to run?

Platform control Apple vs Adobe Ruffle emulator Flashpoint Archive
03
Cultural Archaeology

What We Actually Lost

The technical history of platform death is documented. What is not documented — and what this project contributes — is the cultural inventory of what disappeared.

Lost Aesthetic Forms
GeoCities' tiled backgrounds, animated GIFs, guestbook culture — the first democratic visual language of the web, created by non-designers
Vine's 6-second comedic grammar — timing, callback, loop as structure — an entirely original narrative form with no successor
Flash's interactive experimental artworks — browser games, animated shorts, interactive fiction that cannot be run today without specialist tools
MSN Messenger's "personal message" — the first mass practice of the micro-status update, direct predecessor of the tweet and the Instagram bio
MySpace's profile HTML customisation — the only major platform that ever allowed users to redesign their own page completely
Lost Social Worlds
GeoCities' themed neighborhoods — the first attempt to organise the web spatially around community identity, anticipating Reddit and Facebook Groups
14 million independent musicians' entire recorded output on MySpace — no backup, no recovery, no accountability
The Vine creator community — predominantly Black and brown artists whose innovations were absorbed by TikTok without credit or compensation
MSN Messenger conversation histories — billions of exchanges that formed the first written record of adolescent digital sociality in history
Newgrounds' ecosystem — a generation of animators, game designers, and musicians who trained on Flash, now without a public portfolio
04
Timeline of Loss

A Calendar of Erasure

1999
Yahoo acquires GeoCities for $3.57 billion. The beginning of the end for the web's first democratic publishing platform. The acquisition logic: the community has value, but the community is not the point.
2009
GeoCities shut down (October 26). 38 million user pages deleted overnight. The Archive Team races to save what it can — mostly succeeding technically. But the neighborhoods, the community, the context: gone.
2010
Steve Jobs publishes "Thoughts on Flash." The death warrant for the technology that made the early web creative. Within a decade, Flash-based works become inaccessible in all major browsers — an entire era of creative culture rendered unreachable.
2013
Microsoft kills MSN Messenger, migrating users to Skype. Billions of conversations — the first mass written record of digital adolescent sociality — are not archived. Twitter acquires Vine in October for $30 million.
2017
Vine shuts down (January 17). A partial archive at vine.co is preserved, but the algorithmic discovery, the trending culture, the community itself: dissolved. Creators watch TikTok replicate their innovations — without credit.
2019
MySpace confirms loss of 50 million songs during a server migration. No warning. No recovery. The largest single act of musical erasure in history, performed not by war or censorship but by a botched IT migration. Google+ also closes in April.
2020
Adobe Flash End of Life (December 31). All major browsers block Flash content. An estimated 2–3 million Flash-based artworks, games, and interactive works become inaccessible without specialist emulators like Ruffle.
2026
This project published. Some of this history is being written for the first time, from a humanistic perspective — by a team at the Universidad de Deusto, following the call of the DH Manifesto 2.0 to produce new scholarship, not Wikipedia translations.

Our Position

What We Believe

Following the DH Manifesto 2.0's call for position-taking and not merely description — here is ours.

A MINI-MANIFESTO ON PLATFORM DEATH

  1. Digital culture is culture. The conversations on MSN, the songs on MySpace, the animations on Newgrounds, the pages on GeoCities — these are as worthy of preservation as any archive of print, film, or broadcast media.
  2. Platform owners do not own the cultures their platforms host. The terms of service said they did. The terms of service were wrong.
  3. The decision to shut a platform is a public decision. It should not be made unilaterally by a corporation. It has public consequences and should require public accountability, public consultation, and public archiving obligations.
  4. Curation is scholarship. Following the DH Manifesto 2.0: archiving dead platforms is not nostalgia. This project is scholarship. Writing humanistic analysis that does not yet exist is a contribution to knowledge.
  5. Enshittification is a business model, not a law of nature. Co-operatively owned platforms, non-profit infrastructure, and open protocols are possible. The death of these platforms is the predictable outcome of a specific economic logic — not an inevitability.
  6. The creators deserve credit. The Black creators on Vine who invented the dances TikTok monetised. The musicians on MySpace whose recordings are now gone. The teenagers on MSN whose first attempts at self-expression were treated as worthless data.

Methodology

How We Did This

Our methodology combines digital archival research, cultural analysis, and humanistic close reading. Each step is documented and reproducible by other researchers.

01
Platform Selection

Each team member selected one platform based on cultural significance, degree of cultural loss, and availability of sources. Priority given to platforms where the human cost of deletion is under-documented.

02
Source Archaeology

Research used: the Internet Archive (archive.org), the Archive Team's GeoCities torrent, academic platform studies, journalism, and first-person accounts from former users in community forums and Reddit archives.

03
Cultural Analysis

Three lenses applied to every platform: enshittification (Doctorow), data extractivism (Cartography of GenAI), and curation as scholarship (DH Manifesto 2.0). Focus: what aesthetic forms emerged, who created them, who profited, and who decided to end it.

04
Web Publication

Research published as this open-access website, freely readable and shareable under CC BY-SA 4.0. The site is itself a demonstration of DH practice: using digital tools to produce and disseminate humanistic knowledge publicly.
